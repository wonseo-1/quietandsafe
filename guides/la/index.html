77th Street

77th Street refers to a broad South Los Angeles area centered on established residential neighborhoods and local commercial corridors. The area reflects the city's Black and Latino cultural heritage, with churches, schools, parks, and community institutions anchoring daily life.

Central

Central Los Angeles centers on downtown and adjacent historic districts, the city's civic, cultural, and transit hub. Towering offices, older commercial buildings, markets, entertainment venues, and rapidly changing residential areas sit close together.

Devonshire

Devonshire covers much of the northwestern San Fernando Valley, where suburban residential neighborhoods meet shopping centers, campuses, and foothill landscapes. Northridge and nearby communities give the area a spacious, valley-oriented character.

Foothill

Foothill covers northeastern San Fernando Valley communities near the base of the San Gabriel Mountains. It is largely residential, with suburban commercial centers, industrial pockets, and easy access to mountain-front open space.

Harbor

Harbor encompasses the city's southern port communities, including San Pedro and Wilmington. Its identity is inseparable from the Port of Los Angeles, maritime industry, working waterfronts, and diverse residential neighborhoods set well apart from central Los Angeles.

Hollenbeck

Hollenbeck encompasses historic Eastside communities near downtown, including much of Boyle Heights. Its character is rooted in multigenerational Latino culture, residential streets, small businesses, and proximity to major civic and transportation corridors.

Hollywood

Hollywood is an internationally recognized entertainment district, associated with film history, theaters, music, tourism, and the Hollywood Sign. Behind its visitor-facing landmarks, it also contains dense apartment neighborhoods, nightlife, production activity, and longstanding residential communities.

Mission

Mission covers northern San Fernando Valley communities, including areas around Mission Hills and Sylmar. It combines residential neighborhoods, commercial strips, schools, and foothill-adjacent landscapes, with a more suburban rhythm than central Los Angeles.

N Hollywood

North Hollywood is a San Fernando Valley neighborhood known for the NoHo Arts District, theaters, studios, and transit connections. Beyond its cultural core, it combines apartment areas, single-family blocks, and a steadily evolving commercial scene.

Newton

Newton covers neighborhoods south of downtown where residential blocks, warehouses, garment-related businesses, and long-established Latino communities overlap. Its central location places it near major transit and employment corridors while retaining a strongly local character.

Northeast

Northeast Los Angeles is a hilly, eclectic area northeast of downtown, encompassing communities such as Highland Park, Eagle Rock, and Lincoln Heights. It blends historic housing, independent businesses, creative energy, and pockets of open space.

Olympic

Olympic spans central Los Angeles neighborhoods west of downtown, with a mix of apartments, small businesses, and major east-west activity. Its varied landscape includes portions of Koreatown, Mid-Wilshire, and surrounding multicultural communities.

Pacific

Pacific represents Los Angeles's coastal southwest, encompassing beach-oriented communities with a relaxed outdoor culture, visitor activity, and strong ties to the ocean. Its identity is shaped by shoreline recreation, aviation-related employment, and neighborhood commercial districts.

Rampart

Rampart covers a dense stretch just northwest of downtown, where apartment-lined corridors, longtime immigrant communities, and busy commercial streets create an urban, highly walkable feel. It includes parts of Koreatown, Westlake, and nearby central neighborhoods.

Southeast

Southeast Los Angeles is a broad area of residential neighborhoods, industrial zones, and local commercial corridors. Its communities have deep roots in the city's Black and Latino cultural life and remain closely connected to regional employment centers.

Southwest

Southwest Los Angeles includes residential communities south and west of downtown, where modest homes, apartments, local businesses, and longstanding civic institutions define the streetscape. It is a broad, diverse area with strong neighborhood-level identities.

Topanga

Topanga is a canyon community in the Santa Monica Mountains, west of the city proper, known for a rural, arts-oriented character and winding hillside setting. It feels distinct from Los Angeles's denser urban neighborhoods.

Van Nuys

Van Nuys is a central San Fernando Valley neighborhood with a busy commercial spine, diverse apartment and single-family areas, and a long association with aviation and light industry. It serves as an everyday service and transportation hub for surrounding communities.

West LA

West LA is a compact Westside neighborhood between Santa Monica and the central city, marked by apartments, offices, restaurants, and busy boulevards. Its location near major job centers and the coast supports a lively, connected feel.

West Valley

West Valley refers to the western San Fernando Valley, a largely suburban expanse of residential neighborhoods, shopping districts, and employment centers. Woodland Hills, Canoga Park, and nearby communities contribute varied identities within its broad footprint.
