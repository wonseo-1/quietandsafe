ARPT

ARPT refers to the area surrounding Harry Reid International Airport, shaped by hotels, rental-car facilities, aviation-related businesses, and major transportation links. It is primarily a gateway zone rather than a traditional residential neighborhood.

BAC

BAC represents the historic West Las Vegas area, home to longstanding neighborhoods, community institutions, and locally rooted businesses. Its identity reflects the city’s African American history and its role in Las Vegas civic life.

CCAC

CCAC encompasses the Convention Center area, where large meetings, trade shows, hotels, and entertainment create a distinctly visitor-oriented atmosphere. It blends major tourism infrastructure with nearby residential and commercial blocks.

DTAC

DTAC centers on downtown Las Vegas, the city’s historic urban core. Fremont Street, older casinos, civic buildings, arts venues, and redevelopment projects give the area a lively mix of heritage and contemporary activity.

EAC

EAC covers Enterprise in the southwest valley, an area of rapid growth combining residential communities, resort-oriented development, warehouses, and shopping districts. It connects suburban Las Vegas with the airport and southern Strip vicinity.

NEAC

NEAC serves northeastern Las Vegas, an expansive area with long-established residential communities alongside newer development. Its identity is largely suburban, with local shopping, schools, and broad views across the valley.

NWAC

NWAC covers much of northwest Las Vegas, where newer suburban development meets established residential communities. The area is known for master-planned neighborhoods, shopping centers, parks, and access to the valley’s northern and western edges.

SAC

SAC covers the Summerlin area on the valley’s western side, known for master-planned residential villages, shopping and dining centers, and access to outdoor recreation near the foothills.

SCAC

SCAC represents south-central Las Vegas, a mixed urban area of established neighborhoods, apartments, businesses, and visitor-serving corridors. Its character is shaped by proximity to the Strip and central-valley employment centers.

SEAC

SEAC covers southeastern Las Vegas, a broad area of residential neighborhoods, commercial centers, and newer development. It offers a suburban character while remaining closely connected to major employment, shopping, and transportation routes.

SVAC

SVAC includes the Spring Valley area west of the Strip, a densely developed mix of single-family homes, apartments, restaurants, and retail. It is a practical, centrally located residential area with strong everyday commercial activity.
